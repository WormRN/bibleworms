Bible basics

Rules for Reading the Bible Well

The Bible is not meant to be a locked book for experts only. But we do need a few guardrails — because the goal of Bible study is not to make the Bible say what we already think.

You do not need a seminary degree to begin. You do not need to know Greek or Hebrew to hear God speak through Scripture. You can start with a humble heart, a readable Bible, a few good questions, and a willingness to listen.

But we do need a few guardrails.

Why? Because the goal of Bible study is not to make the Bible say what we already think.

The goal is to hear what God has actually said.

These rules help us slow down, pay attention, and read Scripture with faithfulness.

Part one

Fourteen rules for reading well.

Rule 1

Pray for Help

We do not read the Bible alone.

God gave us His Word, and God gives us His Spirit to help us understand, believe, and obey it.

Before you study, pray something simple:

“Lord, help me see what is true. Help me understand what you are saying. Help me trust you and respond.”

This does not mean we can ignore careful reading. The Holy Spirit does not lead us away from the text. He helps us pay attention to it, receive it, and apply it.

Rule 2

Ask, “What Did the Author Mean?”

A Bible passage does not mean whatever we want it to mean.

Our first question is not, “What does this mean to me?” Our first question is, “What did the author mean?”

God spoke through real human authors. They wrote real words to real people in real situations. So we want to understand what the author was trying to communicate.

After we understand what the passage meant, then we can ask how it applies to us today.

Meaning comes before application.
Rule 3

Read the Bible Normally

Read the Bible the way it asks to be read.

If a passage is history, read it as history. If it is poetry, read it as poetry. If it is a letter, read it like a letter. If it uses a symbol, read it as a symbol.

Do not hunt for secret codes or hidden meanings. Do not turn every detail into an allegory. Start with the plain, normal sense of the words.

Sometimes the Bible uses figures of speech. Jesus is not literally a wooden door when He says, “I am the door.” But He really is the only way into salvation and safety.

Read carefully. Read naturally. Let the passage tell you what kind of language it is using.
Rule 4

Read the Verse in Context

Never build a belief from a verse ripped out of its home.

Every verse lives inside a sentence. Every sentence lives inside a paragraph. Every paragraph lives inside a book. Every book lives inside the whole Bible.

Before asking, “How does this verse apply to my life?” ask: What comes before this? What comes after this? Who is speaking? Who is being spoken to? What is the main point of this section?

A verse can be true and still be misused when it is pulled away from its context.
Rule 5

Let Clear Passages Help Explain Hard Passages

The Bible does not contradict itself.

Some passages are clear. Some are harder to understand. When we come to a difficult passage, we should not build a strange belief from it while ignoring clearer teaching elsewhere.

Let the clear help explain the unclear.

This does not mean we pretend hard passages are easy. It means we read the whole Bible together, trusting that God’s Word is unified.

Rule 6

Pay Attention to Words and Sentences

Words matter.

When you study a passage, notice repeated words, connecting words, commands, promises, warnings, and contrasts.

Words like therefore, because, but, if, so that, and for can carry a lot of meaning.

It can also help to compare a few trustworthy Bible translations. Sometimes one translation makes the sentence easier to understand. Another may show the structure more clearly.

Most beginners do not need to do original-language word studies. But when you do use Bible dictionaries or study tools, be careful.

A word does not mean every possible thing it has ever meant. It means what it means in that sentence, in that context.
Rule 7

Learn the Original Setting

The Bible was written for us, but it was not first written to us.

That means we need to ask about the original setting. Who wrote this? Who first received it? What was happening at the time? What cultural details might help us understand the passage?

This matters because the Bible happened in real places, real cultures, and real moments in history.

Understanding the original setting helps us cross the bridge from their world to ours.
Rule 8

Keep Jesus at the Center

The whole Bible points us toward Jesus.

That does not mean every verse is secretly about Jesus in a forced or strange way. It means the whole story of Scripture moves toward God’s rescue plan through Christ.

The Old Testament prepares for Him. The Gospels reveal Him. The letters explain life in Him. Revelation shows His final victory.

So when we read any part of the Bible, we ask: Where does this fit in God’s big story? How does this passage show our need for God’s grace? How does it point us toward Christ, His kingdom, or the life He gives?

The Bible is not mainly a book of heroes to copy. It is the story of the God who saves.
Rule 9

Know Where You Are in the Bible’s Story

Not every command in the Bible is given directly to every person in the same way.

Some commands were given to Israel under the Old Covenant. Some instructions were given to the church under the New Covenant. Some promises were given to specific people in specific moments.

We need to ask: Where are we in the Bible’s story? Is this before or after Jesus’ death and resurrection? Is this under the Old Covenant or the New Covenant? Is this command repeated or renewed for Christians?

This helps us honor the whole Bible without flattening the whole Bible.
Rule 10

Ask Whether the Passage Describes or Commands

Some passages describe what happened. Other passages command what should happen. That difference matters.

David had many wives. That does not mean we should copy David. Peter denied Jesus. That does not mean denial is faithful. The early church shared generously. That does show us something beautiful about Spirit-shaped community.

A story may show us wisdom, warning, courage, failure, faith, or grace. But not every action in a story is an example to follow.

Ask: Is this passage describing an event? Is it giving a command? Is the person in the story being praised, corrected, or simply reported? Does another part of Scripture clearly teach this as something we should do?

Rule 11

Separate Timeless Truth from Cultural Expression

Some biblical commands are timeless and direct. Love your neighbor. Do not steal. Forgive one another. Flee sexual immorality. Worship God alone.

Other commands include cultural details from the ancient world. In those cases, we look for the lasting principle and ask how to live it faithfully today.

For example, a command about hospitality may look different in different cultures, but the call to welcome others remains.

We should not dismiss a command just because it feels ancient. But we should also be careful to understand what part is the timeless truth and what part is the cultural form.
Rule 12

Use Good Reasoning

Bible study requires careful thinking.

Sometimes Scripture teaches something directly. Other times, we draw a reasonable conclusion from several biblical truths. That is okay, but we should be humble.

A direct statement carries more weight than a possible inference. A clear command carries more weight than a guess. A major theme carries more weight than an isolated detail.

Good interpretation does not turn imagination into doctrine.
Rule 13

Apply the Bible After You Understand It

Application matters deeply. We do not study the Bible just to collect information. But application comes after interpretation.

We should not start with our feelings, experiences, or opinions and then force the Bible to support them. Instead, we let Scripture examine us.

Ask: What does this passage teach about God? What does it reveal about people? What does it show about sin, grace, faith, obedience, or hope? What needs to change in my thinking, trusting, loving, or living?

The Bible is not just something we inspect. It is something God uses to inspect us.
Rule 14

Read with Humility and with the Church

You can read the Bible for yourself, but you should not read it by yourself in isolation.

God has given teachers, pastors, mature believers, and the historic church to help us. We should listen carefully, test everything by Scripture, and stay humble.

If your interpretation is brand new, totally private, and no faithful Christian in history seems to have seen it before, slow down.

That does not automatically mean you are wrong, but it should make you careful.

Bible study is personal, but it is not private invention.
Part two

How to read different kinds of passages.

Different parts of the Bible need to be read in different ways. We do not read Psalms exactly like Romans. We do not read Proverbs exactly like Revelation. The Bible is one unified story, but it contains different kinds of writing.

Genre

Letters in the New Testament

Books like Romans, Galatians, Ephesians, Philippians, James, and 1 Peter are letters. They were written to real churches or real people in real situations.

How to read it:

  • Ask what problem, question, or situation the letter is addressing.
  • Pay attention to the flow of thought. Letters often build an argument step by step.
  • Look for commands, explanations, corrections, encouragements, and gospel promises.
When our situation is similar to theirs, the instruction usually applies directly. When our situation is different, look for the lasting truth underneath the specific instruction.
Genre

Old Testament Stories

Books like Genesis, Exodus, Joshua, Judges, Ruth, Samuel, Kings, Ezra, Nehemiah, and Esther contain many historical stories. These stories are true, but they are not just hero tales.

How to read it:

  • Remember that God is the main character.
  • Look for what the story reveals about God’s faithfulness, human weakness, sin, judgment, mercy, promise, and rescue.
  • Do not assume every character is an example to copy. Some people in Bible stories show us faith. Some show us failure. Many show us both.
Stories often illustrate truth, but they do not always directly teach doctrine by themselves. Use clearer teaching passages to help you understand what a story is showing.
Genre

Acts and the Early Church

Acts tells the story of Jesus continuing His work through the Holy Spirit and the early church. It shows the gospel spreading from Jerusalem to the nations.

How to read it:

  • Pay attention to the main point of the story, not just the side details.
  • Ask what Luke, the author, is trying to show.
  • Notice what is repeated and emphasized.
Not everything that happened in Acts is automatically a command for every church to copy in the exact same way. To know the difference, ask whether the passage itself presents the event as something all Christians should continue doing.
Genre

The Gospels

Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John tell us about Jesus’ life, teaching, death, and resurrection. They are not random collections of Jesus stories. Each Gospel writer carefully presents Jesus for a purpose.

How to read it:

  • Read each Gospel on its own terms. Notice what the writer emphasizes.
  • When the same event appears in more than one Gospel, compare the accounts. The differences are not mistakes. They often show what each writer wants us to notice.
  • Pay attention to both settings: the setting of Jesus’ earthly ministry, and the setting of the Gospel writer teaching the church about Jesus.
Read Jesus’ commands through the gospel of grace. Jesus is not giving us a way to earn salvation. He is showing us the life of His kingdom. We obey not to make God love us, but because in Christ we are already loved.
Genre

Parables

Parables are short stories Jesus told to reveal truth, expose hearts, and call for a response. They are simple stories, but they are not always soft stories.

How to read it:

  • Ask who Jesus is speaking to.
  • Look for the surprise, twist, or unexpected turn.
  • Ask what response Jesus wanted from the original hearers.
  • Do not turn every detail into a hidden symbol.
Most parables have one main point or a small cluster of main points. Stay focused on what Jesus is pressing into the hearts of His listeners.
Genre

The Old Testament Law

The Law was given to Israel under the Old Covenant. It taught Israel how to live as God’s covenant people. It shaped their worship, justice, purity, sacrifices, calendar, and national life.

How to read it:

  • Remember that Christians are under the New Covenant, not the Old Covenant.
  • Jesus fulfilled the Law. We do not obey the civil and ceremonial laws of Israel as if we lived under that covenant.
  • But the Law still teaches us. It reveals God’s holiness. It exposes sin. It shows the need for sacrifice. It teaches wisdom and justice. It points us to Christ.
Do not apply Old Testament laws to Christians automatically. Ask whether the command is renewed, fulfilled, transformed, or set aside in the New Testament. The moral heart of God’s will continues, but we read the Law through Jesus and the New Covenant.
Genre

The Prophets

Books like Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Hosea, Amos, Micah, and Malachi contain messages from God through His prophets. The prophets were not mainly fortune-tellers. They were covenant messengers.

How to read it:

  • Try to identify the specific message, audience, and situation.
  • Many prophetic books are collections of shorter messages. Do not assume the whole book is one speech given at one time.
  • Look for themes of sin, judgment, repentance, mercy, restoration, and hope.
  • Pay attention to poetry and imagery.
Do not invent your own second meaning for a prophecy. Sometimes the New Testament shows us a deeper fulfillment in Christ. When it does, we receive that gladly. But we should not create hidden fulfillments on our own authority.
Genre

Psalms and Poetry

The Psalms are prayers, songs, poems, laments, praises, confessions, and cries for help. They teach us how to bring our whole life before God.

How to read it:

  • Read with your heart awake. Notice images, emotions, repetition, contrast, and movement.
  • Ask what kind of psalm you are reading: lament, praise, thanksgiving, wisdom, confession, royal, or trust.
  • Read the whole psalm, not just one favorite line.
Poetry uses picture language. Do not flatten it into wooden literalism. When the psalm says God is a rock, it does not mean God is made of stone. It means He is strong, steady, safe, and dependable.
Genre

Proverbs and Wisdom Literature

Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, and Song of Songs help us grow in wisdom. They teach us how to live skillfully before God in the real world.

How to read it:

  • Proverbs are usually general truths, not mechanical guarantees. They describe the way life normally works under God’s wise design.
  • For example, Proverbs teaches that diligence usually leads to provision and laziness usually leads to lack. But the rest of the Bible also shows that righteous people can suffer and wicked people can prosper for a time.
Do not treat one proverb as the whole truth about every situation. Read Proverbs as a collection. Balance one proverb with another. Also remember that some proverbs describe how life works in a fallen world without approving of everything they describe.
Genre

Revelation and Apocalyptic Writing

Revelation is a unique book. It includes letters, prophecy, and apocalyptic imagery. It uses symbols, numbers, visions, beasts, dragons, thrones, seals, trumpets, bowls, and cosmic conflict. This can feel confusing at first, but the main message is not confusing: Jesus wins. God reigns. Evil will be judged. The suffering church will be vindicated. God will make all things new.

How to read it:

  • Expect symbols. Ask what the image communicates before trying to force every detail into a newspaper headline.
  • Pay attention to Old Testament echoes. Revelation is filled with images from earlier Scripture.
  • Stay focused on worship, endurance, holiness, witness, and hope.
Do not get lost in date-setting or elaborate end-times charts. Revelation was written to strengthen suffering believers, not to feed curiosity. The point is not fear. The point is faithfulness.
In summary

A simple summary.

Pray before you read. Look for what the author meant. Read the verse in context. Pay attention to the kind of writing. Let clear passages guide hard passages. Keep Jesus at the center. Apply the Bible after you understand it. Read with humility, obedience, and help from the church.

You do not have to master everything at once. Start small. Stay honest. Keep showing up.

The goal is not to become impressive. The goal is to meet God in His Word and be formed by what He says.

Put the guardrails to work.
