Which Bible Translation Should I Use?

A simple guide to why translations differ and how to choose one.

Walk into a bookstore or open a Bible app, and the number of choices can feel overwhelming.

KJV. NIV. ESV. NLT. CSB. NASB. NET. NRSVue. BSB. The Message.

Why are there so many?

Did the Bible change? Is one translation accurate while the others are not? Should you choose the version that sounds most literal, the one that reads most naturally, or the one your church uses?

The good news is that you do not need to become a translation expert before you begin reading the Bible.

Most widely used translations are trying to answer the same question. They do not always answer that question in exactly the same way.

Translation is more than replacing words

Imagine that you want to encourage a performer and say:

“Break a leg!”

A translator could reproduce your exact words in another language.

But the listener might think you were threatening them.

Or the translator could communicate what you meant:

“Good luck. I hope you do well.”

Which is more accurate?

The first preserves the individual words. The second preserves the message.

That is the challenge every translator faces.

Languages do not match one another word for word. They use different grammar, sentence order, figures of speech, and cultural expressions. A phrase that makes perfect sense in one language may sound confusing or ridiculous in another.

A translation can preserve every familiar dictionary meaning and still fail to communicate what the speaker intended.

Every translation makes choices

People often describe Bible translations as either “word-for-word” or “thought-for-thought.” Scholars may use terms such as “formal equivalence” or “dynamic equivalence.”

Those labels can be useful in technical discussions, but they can also mislead ordinary readers.

No responsible English translation simply replaces each Hebrew or Greek word with one English word. That would not work.

Even translations known for staying close to the original wording must change word order, supply words required by English grammar, and explain idioms that would otherwise make no sense.

For example, Exodus 34:6 describes God with a Hebrew expression that literally means “long of nose.” Almost no English Bible translates it that way.

Why not?

Because an English reader would not understand it. The phrase communicates the idea that God is slow to become angry. So translations say things such as:

  • slow to anger;
  • longsuffering;
  • patient.

That is not careless translation. It is translation doing its job.

On the other hand, some passages are very straightforward. First John 1:9 says that if we confess our sins, God is faithful and just to forgive and cleanse us. Most translations sound very similar because the original wording is clear and natural to express in English.

Translations differ most when the original sentence is difficult, the wording is ambiguous, or an ancient figure of speech does not carry easily into modern English.

“What it says” and “what it means”

Instead of treating translations as either literal or loose, it may be more helpful to picture a spectrum.

Closer to what it says

These translations tend to preserve more of the original wording, grammar, sentence structure, metaphors, and ambiguity.

  • They can make repeated words and sentence structure easier to notice.
  • They may sound less natural in English.
  • They can be especially useful for close comparison and detailed study.

Closer to what it means

These translations tend to express the intended meaning in clearer, more natural English.

  • They may explain difficult expressions or make implied meaning clearer.
  • They can make long passages easier to follow.
  • They can be especially helpful for regular reading and listening.

Very close to form Interlinear Berean Literal NASB LSB
Close to form ESV NRSVue KJV NKJV
Balanced Berean Standard CSB NIV NET
Closer to meaning NLT GNT
Paraphrase The Message The Living Bible

This is only an approximate guide. Translations move along the spectrum from passage to passage. A version that reads very closely in one verse may explain more freely in the next.

The scale is not a ranking from best to worst. It simply shows different translation habits.

What about the King James Version?

The King James Version deserves respect.

It has shaped English-speaking Christianity for centuries. Its language has influenced preaching, worship, literature, and the way generations of Christians learned Scripture.

It can also be very accurate.

Its main difficulty is not that it is a bad Bible. Its difficulty is that English has changed.

Words that were clear centuries ago may now be confusing or may mean something different. A reader may think they understand a sentence while missing what the older English once communicated.

Someone who has read the KJV for many years and understands it well does not need to stop using it.

But it is usually not the best starting place for a newcomer who is trying to understand the Bible clearly.

The purpose of reading Scripture is not to prove that we can manage older English. It is to hear what God has said.

What about The Message?

The Message should be understood for what Eugene Peterson intended it to be.

He did not create it as the main text for technical study. He wanted people who had become overly familiar with biblical language to hear Scripture freshly again.

It is highly interpretive and written in vivid, contemporary English. That can be a strength.

It can help a familiar passage feel alive. It can show how a text may land emotionally or conversationally. It can be especially helpful for devotional reading alongside another translation.

But it should not usually be your only Bible for careful study.

Use it to hear a passage afresh, then compare it with a trusted committee translation.

Which translation should I use?

The best choice depends partly on what you are doing.

For comfortable reading

Translations such as these often read smoothly:

  • NLT
  • NIV
  • BSB
  • CSB

They can be helpful for reading whole chapters, following a Bible-reading plan, listening to Scripture, family reading, new believers, and readers who find long or complex sentences difficult.

For closer comparative study

Translations such as these often keep more of the original structure visible:

  • ESV
  • NASB
  • LSB
  • NRSVue
  • Berean Literal Bible

They can be helpful when you want to notice repeated words, compare sentence structure, examine difficult phrases, trace an argument, or study alongside commentaries and language tools.

For one Bible that does both well

These translations often balance readability with close attention to the original text:

  • NIV
  • CSB
  • Berean Standard Bible
  • NET

That does not mean they are perfect or that everyone must choose one of them. It simply means they often work well for both regular reading and serious study.

You may also want to ask a pastor, a Bible study leader, or an experienced Christian who actually reads and studies the Bible. Ask what they use and why.

Compare translations when you study

One of the simplest ways to study more carefully is to compare several translations.

Psalm 23:1 provides a good example:

  • “I shall not want.”
  • “I lack nothing.”
  • “I have what I need.”
  • “I have all that I need.”

The wording changes, but each translation is expressing the same central truth: because the Lord is David’s Shepherd, he is not left without what he truly needs.

When translations differ, do not panic. Ask:

  • What do they agree on?
  • What does each wording help me notice?
  • Is there an idiom or difficult phrase here?
  • Is one translation making something explicit that another leaves implied?

Differences can become windows into the text.

Using an online parallel Bible

A parallel Bible displays the same verse in several translations side by side.

Bible Hub offers this function. On the site, look for “Par.”

  1. Search for a verse.
  2. Select “Par.”
  3. Read three to five translations.
  4. Notice what stays the same.
  5. Pay attention to meaningful differences.

Do not assume that the most unusual wording must be the deepest or most accurate. Often the clearest help comes from seeing where several reliable translations agree.

A few important cautions

Prefer committee translations

Translations produced by teams of qualified scholars usually provide more checks and balances.

A single translator may offer valuable insight, but one person’s preferences, theology, and blind spots have less correction. That does not make every single-person translation worthless. It does mean we should read it with appropriate caution and compare it with committee translations.

New World Translation

We strongly recommend avoiding the New World Translation.

It was produced by Jehovah’s Witnesses and reflects their theology at important passages, especially those concerning the identity and nature of Jesus.

This is not merely a difference in translation style. It affects foundational Christian belief.

The Passion Translation

We do not recommend The Passion Translation as a primary Bible or as the main text for careful study.

It is highly interpretive and closely associated with one lead translator. It may sometimes sound vivid or devotional, but readers should compare it with reliable committee translations and avoid building doctrine from its distinctive wording.

Paraphrases

Paraphrases can help familiar passages sound fresh.

They are best used beside a trusted translation, not instead of one.

Do not turn translation choice into a battle

Christians sometimes become fiercely loyal to one translation. That can create more heat than light.

No English translation is the original Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek. Every English Bible is an attempt to carry God’s Word across languages and cultures.

Some do that by keeping more of the original form visible. Others do it by making more of the meaning immediately clear.

Both approaches can serve the church.

Read widely when you study. Read naturally when you want to hear the flow. Use good tools. Ask questions. Stay humble.

Most importantly, open the Bible.

A translation cannot shape you while it remains closed.

Compare translations as you study.
